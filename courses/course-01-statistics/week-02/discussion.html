Week 2 Discussion Topics

Explore how random variables and probability distributions are used in machine learning and AI systems.

Topic 1: The Ubiquity of the Normal Distribution

The Normal (Gaussian) distribution appears everywhere in nature and machine learning—from measurement errors to weight initializations in neural networks.

  • Why do so many real-world phenomena follow approximately Normal distributions? (Hint: Think about the Central Limit Theorem, which we'll cover in Week 6)
  • When might assuming Normality be dangerous or inappropriate in ML applications?
  • How do neural network weight initializations (like Xavier/He initialization) leverage properties of the Normal distribution?

Topic 2: Discrete vs. Continuous in Practice

In theory, discrete and continuous random variables are distinct. In practice, the line often blurs.

  • Digital computers represent all numbers discretely. Does this mean there's no such thing as a truly continuous random variable in computational practice?
  • When might you model inherently discrete data (like counts) with continuous distributions? What are the trade-offs?
  • How do generative AI models (like diffusion models) handle the transition between discrete tokens and continuous representations?

Topic 3: Choosing the Right Distribution

Selecting an appropriate distribution to model data is a critical skill in applied statistics and ML.

  • What criteria would you use to select between Poisson, Negative Binomial, and Zero-Inflated Poisson for count data?
  • In machine learning, we often use specific distributions for specific tasks (Bernoulli for binary classification, Categorical for multi-class). Why do these choices matter?
  • How might misspecifying the distribution affect an ML model's predictions and uncertainty estimates?

Topic 4: The Role of CDFs in ML

While PDFs and PMFs get more attention, CDFs have important applications in ML and statistics.

  • How are CDFs used in goodness-of-fit tests like the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test?
  • Quantile regression predicts conditional quantiles rather than means. How does this relate to the CDF, and when might it be preferable to standard regression?
  • The inverse CDF (quantile function) is crucial for sampling. Explain how you would generate samples from any distribution given only its CDF.
